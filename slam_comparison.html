This project explores the performance and accuracy of two prominent SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) algorithms—GMapping and Cartographer—in indoor environments. SLAM is crucial for autonomous vehicles as it enables them to create maps and localize themselves within unfamiliar surroundings. In our study, we compared GMapping, which employs a 2D grid-based particle filter approach, and Cartographer, which utilizes a graph-based method capable of both 2D and 3D mapping.

Using an RPLIDAR sensor, we collected real-time data to assess how each algorithm performed in controlled environments with varying complexities. Our findings revealed that GMapping is better suited for simpler environments with fewer obstacles, whereas Cartographer excels in more complex scenarios. We faced challenges in quantitatively comparing the SLAM algorithms due to the absence of standardized metrics and issues such as LIDAR blind spots, which underscored the need for more nuanced evaluation methods. Moving forward, we propose integrating additional sensors and employing depth cameras to enhance obstacle detection and overcome some of the limitations identified. This research contributes to developing more robust autonomous systems, emphasizing the vital role of SLAM in modern robotics.
